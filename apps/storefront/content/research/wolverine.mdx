<MolecularStructure src="wolverine" />

Wolverine is a proprietary preclinical research blend combining two well-characterized peptide compounds: BPC-157 (Body Protection Compound-157), a synthetic 15-amino acid pentadecapeptide derived from a partial sequence of human gastric juice protein, and GHK-Cu (glycine-L-histidine-L-lysine copper complex), a naturally occurring tripeptide-copper chelate found in human plasma, saliva, and urine. The rationale for this combination lies in the complementary and potentially synergistic mechanisms of the two components: BPC-157 operates primarily through growth factor upregulation and nitric oxide system modulation, while GHK-Cu engages copper-dependent signaling cascades governing collagen synthesis, extracellular matrix remodeling, and antioxidant defense.

In non-clinical models, the two constituent peptides have independently demonstrated broad tissue-supportive activity across overlapping yet mechanistically distinct pathways. Combining them into a single formulation provides researchers with a multi-target experimental tool suitable for studying coordinated tissue repair processes in preclinical systems. The pairing of a pro-angiogenic, cytoprotective peptide (BPC-157) with a matrix-remodeling, copper-dependent signal transducer (GHK-Cu) reflects a logical multi-pathway approach to investigating tissue support in experimental settings.

## Mechanism of Action

The biological activity of Wolverine in experimental systems reflects the combined and potentially complementary mechanisms of its two constituent peptides. BPC-157 engages the VEGF/FGF axis and nitric oxide (NO) system to promote angiogenesis, endothelial integrity, and cellular migration in non-clinical models.<Cite id={1} /> GHK-Cu operates through a distinct copper-mediated signaling arm, activating transcription factors associated with collagen I and III synthesis, MMP regulation, and antioxidant enzyme expression in experimental systems.<Cite id={2} />

In experimental systems, GHK-Cu has been shown to activate the TGF-β signaling pathway, upregulating decorin and other proteoglycans critical for organized extracellular matrix deposition.<Cite id={3} /> Simultaneously, BPC-157's modulation of the FAK-paxillin pathway supports cellular adhesion and migration into repair zones in non-clinical models.<Cite id={4} /> The convergence of these mechanisms — pro-angiogenic growth factor signaling from BPC-157 and copper-driven matrix synthesis from GHK-Cu — provides a multi-target preclinical research rationale for the combination. It should be emphasized that all mechanistic observations derive from in-vitro and non-clinical animal studies.

<ResearchApplications>
<Application title="Combined Tissue Support">
The primary preclinical research rationale for the Wolverine blend is investigation of multi-pathway tissue support in experimental models. Non-clinical studies of BPC-157 and GHK-Cu individually have reported complementary activity: BPC-157 facilitating early vascular ingrowth and cellular migration, and GHK-Cu supporting subsequent matrix consolidation and collagen maturation. Combining these agents in preclinical wound and injury models allows researchers to study coordinated angiogenic and matrix-remodeling responses within the same experimental system.<Cite id={1} /><Cite id={2} />
</Application>

<Application title="Collagen Synthesis Research">
GHK-Cu has been extensively studied in preclinical and in-vitro collagen synthesis models. In experimental fibroblast systems, GHK-Cu treatment was associated with significant upregulation of collagen I and III gene expression, as well as increased synthesis of fibronectin and glycosaminoglycans essential for mature extracellular matrix architecture.<Cite id={3} /> BPC-157 complements this profile by supporting the vascular scaffold required for nutrient delivery to collagen-synthesizing cells in non-clinical wound models, providing a mechanistically rationale combination for collagen research applications.<Cite id={5} />
</Application>

<Application title="Wound Healing Models">
In preclinical wound healing studies, both BPC-157 and GHK-Cu have individually demonstrated activity in experimental excisional and incisional wound models. BPC-157 administration in non-clinical systems has been associated with accelerated reticulin and collagen fiber formation and increased vessel density at wound margins.<Cite id={1} /> GHK-Cu has been associated with enhanced re-epithelialization and reduced wound contraction in non-clinical models, with experimental evidence suggesting modulation of MMP-2 and MMP-9 activity governing matrix turnover during wound remodeling.<Cite id={6} /> The Wolverine blend allows simultaneous investigation of both pathways in wound healing experimental systems.
</Application>

<Application title="Antioxidant Defense Studies">
GHK-Cu's role in copper-dependent antioxidant signaling has been characterized in preclinical and cellular experimental systems. In non-clinical models, GHK-Cu has been associated with upregulation of superoxide dismutase (SOD) and other copper-zinc antioxidant enzymes, reducing oxidative stress markers in experimental ischemia and inflammatory injury models.<Cite id={7} /> BPC-157's interaction with the NO system in non-clinical settings provides a complementary vascular antioxidant dimension, making the Wolverine blend of interest for preclinical research examining multi-mechanism antioxidant responses to experimental tissue injury.
</Application>
</ResearchApplications>

## Handling and Storage Requirements

**Personal Protective Equipment (PPE):** Researchers should wear nitrile gloves, laboratory coat, and safety goggles when handling Wolverine blend in any form. Work in a well-ventilated area or under a fume hood when reconstituting lyophilized material. Note that the GHK-Cu component contains a copper chelate; avoid prolonged skin contact with reconstituted solutions.

**Storage Conditions:** Store lyophilized Wolverine at -20 degrees C in a desiccated environment, protected from light. The copper component of GHK-Cu may undergo oxidation under improper storage conditions; airtight desiccated storage is critical. Reconstituted solutions should be stored at 2-8 degrees C and used within 48 hours, or aliquoted and stored at -20 degrees C to minimize freeze-thaw degradation and copper dissociation.

**Spill Response:** In the event of a spill, contain the area with absorbent material, collect waste into an appropriate chemical waste container, and decontaminate the surface with 70% ethanol followed by water rinse. Dispose of all contaminated materials — including copper-containing fractions — in accordance with institutional chemical and heavy metal waste protocols.

**Documentation Requirements:** Maintain detailed records of lot numbers, reconstitution dates, storage conditions, and experimental usage in compliance with institutional research protocols. All handling must conform to your institution's chemical hygiene plan and applicable regulatory requirements.
