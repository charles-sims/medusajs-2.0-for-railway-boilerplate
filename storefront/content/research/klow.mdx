KLOW is CaliLean's research designation for NAD+ (Nicotinamide Adenine Dinucleotide), an essential dinucleotide coenzyme found in every living cell. NAD+ consists of two nucleotides — adenine and nicotinamide — joined by a phosphate bridge, and exists in two interconvertible redox forms: the oxidized NAD+ and the reduced NADH. First identified by Arthur Harden and William John Young in 1906, NAD+ has since been recognized as one of the most central molecules in cellular metabolism, participating in over 500 enzymatic reactions in non-clinical models.

Beyond its classical role as a hydride-transfer coenzyme in oxidation-reduction reactions, NAD+ functions as a substrate for several classes of signaling enzymes in experimental systems. Intracellular NAD+ concentrations decline measurably with aging in preclinical animal models, a phenotype associated with reduced activity of NAD+-consuming enzymes and diminished mitochondrial function. This age-dependent decline has positioned NAD+ and its biosynthetic precursors as active subjects of preclinical aging research, with studies across multiple non-clinical model organisms reporting that restoration of NAD+ levels influences metabolic and longevity-associated endpoints under experimental conditions.

## Mechanism of Action

NAD+ participates in cellular biology through two principal mechanistic roles in experimental systems. Its classical role involves hydride transfer in catabolic pathways: NAD+ accepts electrons (is reduced to NADH) during glycolysis, the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle, and fatty acid beta-oxidation in non-clinical metabolic models. The resulting NADH is then oxidized back to NAD+ by Complex I of the mitochondrial electron transport chain, driving ATP synthesis via oxidative phosphorylation.<Cite id={1} />

The signaling role of NAD+ centers on its function as the obligate substrate for sirtuin deacylases (SIRT1–7) in experimental systems. Sirtuins catalyze the NAD+-dependent removal of acyl groups from lysine residues on histone and non-histone target proteins, generating nicotinamide and O-acyl-ADP-ribose as byproducts. In non-clinical models, sirtuin activation via elevated NAD+ availability has been associated with deacetylation of PGC-1α (promoting mitochondrial biogenesis), FOXO transcription factors (influencing stress response gene programs), and p53 (modulating DNA damage responses).<Cite id={2} />

NAD+ is also consumed by poly(ADP-ribose) polymerases (PARPs) in DNA repair processes in experimental systems. Upon detection of DNA strand breaks, PARP1 catalyzes the synthesis of poly(ADP-ribose) chains onto acceptor proteins using NAD+ as the ADP-ribose donor, serving as a critical early response mechanism in non-clinical DNA damage models.<Cite id={3} /> This dual NAD+ consumption by sirtuins and PARPs creates a metabolic competition that has been studied extensively in non-clinical aging and genotoxic stress model systems.

<ResearchApplications>
<Application title="Aging Research Models">
Preclinical aging research has extensively characterized NAD+ depletion as a feature of biological aging across multiple non-clinical model organisms including yeast, C. elegans, Drosophila, and rodents. In these experimental systems, supplementation with NAD+ precursors has been associated with improvements in metabolic parameters, muscle function, and lifespan under experimental conditions. These findings are exclusively from non-clinical model systems and do not establish outcomes in humans.<Cite id={4} />
</Application>

<Application title="DNA Repair Studies">
Experimental DNA repair research has investigated NAD+ availability as a rate-limiting factor in PARP-mediated repair responses in non-clinical cell culture and animal systems. Studies in experimental models of genotoxic stress report that NAD+ depletion impairs strand break repair kinetics, while elevated intracellular NAD+ availability in non-clinical systems has been associated with enhanced PARP1 activity and improved resolution of DNA damage foci under experimental conditions.<Cite id={3} />
</Application>

<Application title="Mitochondrial Function Research">
Non-clinical mitochondrial biology studies have characterized the NAD+/NADH ratio as a key regulator of mitochondrial metabolic flux in experimental systems. Preclinical studies using genetic and pharmacological manipulation of NAD+ biosynthesis pathways report bidirectional effects on mitochondrial membrane potential, oxygen consumption rate, and ATP production capacity in non-clinical cell and tissue models. Sirtuin-dependent deacetylation of mitochondrial proteins downstream of NAD+ has been studied as a potential mechanism in these experimental systems.<Cite id={2} />
</Application>

<Application title="Metabolic Pathway Analysis">
Experimental metabolism research has employed NAD+ and its precursors as tool compounds to interrogate the intersection of energy metabolism, circadian rhythm regulation, and cellular senescence in non-clinical models. Preclinical studies report that NAD+-dependent SIRT1 activity modulates the core circadian clock machinery (CLOCK/BMAL1) through deacetylation in experimental systems, providing a mechanistic link between NAD+ metabolism and circadian-regulated metabolic gene programs in non-clinical settings.<Cite id={5} />
</Application>
</ResearchApplications>

## Handling and Storage Requirements

**Personal Protective Equipment (PPE):** Researchers should wear nitrile gloves, laboratory coat, and safety goggles when handling KLOW in any form. NAD+ is a biologically active coenzyme; standard laboratory precautions for biological research compounds apply.

**Storage Conditions:** Store lyophilized NAD+ at -20 degrees C in a desiccated, light-protected environment. NAD+ is susceptible to hydrolytic degradation in aqueous solution; reconstituted solutions should be prepared in cold, pH-neutral buffer, stored at 2-8 degrees C, and used within 24 hours. For extended storage of reconstituted material, aliquot and store at -80 degrees C, minimizing freeze-thaw cycles. Avoid reconstitution in alkaline buffers, as NAD+ hydrolysis is accelerated at elevated pH.

**Spill Response:** Contain spills with absorbent material, collect waste into an appropriate chemical waste container, and decontaminate the surface with 70% ethanol followed by water rinse. Dispose of all contaminated materials in accordance with institutional chemical waste protocols.

**Documentation Requirements:** Maintain detailed records of lot numbers, reconstitution dates, storage conditions, and experimental usage in compliance with institutional research protocols. All handling must conform to your institution's chemical hygiene plan and applicable regulatory requirements.

**This compound is for Research Use Only (RUO). Not for human or veterinary diagnostic or therapeutic use.**
